Mazawattee Tea Company

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The Mazawattee Tea Company was one of the most important and most advertised tea firms in England for around fifty years. Traditionally the origin of tea-drinking lies in China and many people may recall the story of the famous Tea Clipper ships which raced across the seas to bring tea from China to London. In the eighteenth century, tea had become an important drink in Britain especially for the wealthy, but it was not until the 1850s (by which time tea plantations had been successfully established in India, especially in Assam, and Ceylon) that a real expansion occurred. The Densham family were at the forefront of this period of growth and although originally from Plymouth, moved to London and managed to amass a fortune from the business in quite a short time. The Denshams later owned fine properties in both the Purley and Croydon areas and one of the founder’s sons, Edward, became a well-known figure in Purley.

[edit] The growth of tea drinking and the rise of the Densham family

In Britain, the early days of tea drinking were very much a social occasion: it was widely drunk in tea gardens in London perhaps best exemplified by a contemporary short ditty:

Bon Ton’s the space ’twixt Saturday and Monday,
’Tis riding in a one horse chair on Sunday,
’Tis drinking tea on summer afternoons
At Bagnigge Wells with china and gilt spoons.1

But changes were coming to this rather cosy attitude and in Britain, over the years the consumption of tea increased by leaps and bounds. Tea imported for consumption rose from 30 million pounds a year in the 1830s to 80 million lbs. in the 1860s and to 200 million pounds in the 1890s. Companies such as Twinings grew apace and new firms such as Hornimans began to trade around 1840. It was not long before John Boon Densham came to London. He had been a chemist and druggist in Plymouth in the days when it was the practice for these shops to also blend and sell loose-leaf teas. At that time, tea was widely advocated by the Temperance Movement to counteract alcohol consumption and Densham, being a strict Baptist, was probably influenced by this aspect. Whatever his reasons, he set up as a wholesaler in 1865 and then moved to London in his 50s and started trading exclusively in wholesale loose-leaf tea. Perhaps he thought that to set up a business in London would be a splendid way to help his sons thrive. The first name of the firm was Lees & Densham but by 1873 it became Densham & Sons, owned by John Boon (then aged nearly 60) and his three eldest sons Edward, Alfred, and Benjamin who were then in their late 20s/early 30s and were able to contribute their energies to its growth. The firm must have been very successful and the eldest son Edward later took over the main role.

The firm grew without major changes but things altered when the youngest son, John Lane Densham, who was then in his late 20s, was able to join as an employee in 1881. He had not been able to devote himself to daily labour previously owing to poor health but once he joined he soon became the leading light; he was an indefatigable traveller for the firm and his older brothers were soon able to rely on his efforts.

A simple innovation that would later change the whole retail tea industry had already been introduced in 1826 by John Horniman who hit on the idea of packaging tea so that customers could buy a reliable and known brand with confidence and not have to rely on what the grocer selected from his tea-chests. A machine of a simple sort was soon devised to carry out the process more speedily and efficiently. Although packet tea took time to become popular with many people, the Denshams realised that this was the way ahead for retail sales and offered their first packets of Ceylon tea in 1884. By then, other firms had entered the field such as the Co-operative Wholesale Society (1863), Brooke Bond (1869) and Liptons (1888), although it was not until the early 1900s that overall packet tea outstripped loose-leaf tea sales in the grocery shops.

[edit] The origin of Mazawattee tea and the expansion of the firm

The death of John Boon Densham at the age of 72 at his home in Croydon in 1886 ended the first period of the firm’s growth. John Lane Densham was immediately made a partner and tackled the problem of the firm itself. He decided to latch on to supplying all of its tea in packets to retailers and in a different way by inventing a name for the firm to cover these products. Being a great advocate of advertising, he reckoned that something quite unusual might be the answer and went to the Guildhall Library to get ideas. He came up with the idea of using the word “Mazathawattee”, perhaps a rather strange combination based on two words: the Hindi “Mazaa”, which means "pleasure or fun," and the Sinhalese word “vatta," which means "a garden." This was shortened to “Mazawattee” and duly registered as a trade mark for retail sales in May 1887. Of course, tastes continually change over the years and although it may not be an attractive name to us these days, the public took to it then. This was merely a start for he then latched onto the idea of using a standard picture to advertise the brand. It is to many modern eyes a rather gloomy thing showing an aged, bespectacled and somewhat toothless grandmother with her supposed granddaughter and the compulsory cup of tea. This picture also became popular and was used in its original form for many years. Although the artist is not known, the model for the grandmother was a Mary Ann Clarke, the wife of an Islington bootmaker. The model for the child was to have been her granddaughter but she was too shy and the artist had to enlist the aid of the little girl next door, one Alice Emma Nichols. The picture was called “Old Folks at Home”.

The firm no doubt recruited people or used agencies to carry out these advertising schemes and it was not long before the firm’s name was everywhere, stressing the quality of Mazawattee to counteract the criticism of “doctoring” that had been levelled at some teas. A contract was even made with the railway companies so that eye-catching enamelled signs could be fixed on every railway station platform in Britain. Ceylon tea had been introduced to Britain in 1875 and Mazawattee concentrated for some years on this type of tea - in fact, the firm became almost addicted to it - and at one time even named itself the Mazawattee Ceylon Tea Company. High prices were obtained by dealers, especially for the hand-selected leaf buds of good quality tea known as “Golden Tips”. Yet by 1891 this bubble burst and normality returned.

Although the parent company of Densham & Sons handled the loose-leaf trade from 49/51 Eastcheap, by 1894 Mazawattee decided to have its own offices together with warehouses and vaults. A large building was erected at the top of Tower Hill very near the Tower of London. This was so tall that it blocked out the view to the east from the adjacent church of All Hallows by the Tower, certainly not to the liking of the church authorities.2. The Mazawattee building was a perfect venue for viewing big events such as the opening of Tower Bridge on 30 June 1894.

In May 1896 the firm became a public limited company, the Mazawattee Tea Company (Limited), which officially acquired the Mazawattee Ceylon Tea Company and Densham & Sons (the wholesale tea and coffee dealing part of he business) which were thereafter embedded in one big firm. The capital was £550,000 (= £21,400,000 in current terms) in 5% cumulative preference shares of £5 each and 350,000 ordinary shares of £1 each (= £13,625,000 in current terms). The four brothers received in total 13,333 preference shares and 116,666 ordinary shares, a total of £183,333 (= £7,136,000 in current terms). The remainder of 26,667 preference shares and 233,334 ordinary shares were offered for sale. As part of this arrangement and so that the two eldest brothers, Edward and Alfred could retire early, paid £30,000 each (= £1,167,000 in current terms) to John Lane and Benjamin. Inevitably, John Lane Densham soon became chairman of the new company.

[edit] Problems after 1900

Unexpectedly after 1900 things started to go wrong. Tea prices rose, largely due to an increase in duty to help meet the cost of the Boer War (1899-1902), and this was at the same moment as there were strikes and political troubles. Not to be deterred, John Lane Densham kept travelling round the world on behalf of the firm, often taking his family with him, but marred by health problems. He had many interviews with representatives, discussions with agents and visited tea estates in India and Ceylon. Nevertheless, sales began to drop and a scheme was devised to branch out into the cocoa and chocolate trade. This was a risky step to take. A huge site was acquired near New Cross alongside the Surrey Union Canal and a vast building constructed to process and pack products and opened in 1901. The inaugural luncheon was reported favourably in the press for this was a factory built on a grand scale and at one time nearly 2,000 people worked there. For the most part single-storey, it was designed in an impressive and efficient way with a central service corridor. There was a separate tin making and tin printing department. Once again, the future looked encouraging. Publicity continued as ever and typical of the inventiveness of the firm was a small round coin-like commemorative tin containing a chocolate distributed in 1902 to celebrate the coronation of King Edward VII. These may have been given to children as local souvenirs for some of them that have survived have the name of a town added at the bottom of the lid. Another splendid memento of this event was a booklet designed by William Theodore Parkes illustrating the National Anthem with the words and music embellished with ornate emblematic designs; Parkes had started his career in Ireland and exhibited at the Royal Hibernian Academy from 1880 to 1883, after which he worked in London. The booklet is a fine example of period art perhaps reflected in the selling price of one shilling and it managed to introduce the subject of tea and its connection with royalty. Many other fine tins were produced in these years, often celebrating events, and also for other firms. Mazawattee produced other things over the years under various names such as spices and cake flavourings - even a hair tonic.

It was not long before more problems arose; sales declined and the trade in cocoa was not as great as expected. In 1902, John Lane’s health deteriorated and he was obliged to hand over the managing directorship to Robert McQuitty and Alexander Jackson and go abroad. His time was not wasted for he started to arrange for tea to be sent direct from the estates to Russia, America and British Colonies. Events at home almost led to the complete downfall of the Mazawattee business: McQuitty and another director, John McClean, whatever their motives, started a major change in policy, possibly trying to emulate other tea merchant firms that had also become successful retailers. They persuaded John Lane’s brother Benjamin that the way ahead was to open Mazawattee retail shops. They lost no time began to open café-type saloons selling packet tea as a sideline. There was no proper control and they went ahead in a kind of frenzy. Splendid places with colourful tiles and plenty of mahogany fitments started and £10,000 was absorbed by one shop alone, about £400,000 in today’s terms. As part of the scheme, many staff at head office were dismissed including Alexander Jackson. Being close to old-established grocery shops, these new upstart places created opposition, and it was not long before an urgent plea was sent to John Lane Densham beseeching him to return home to rectify the situation. He called an urgent general meeting in March 1906 and one can well visualise the scene there and the drama of having to force the sacking of the two culpable managing directors. Benjamin Densham resigned as chairman and subsequently it was found that McQuitty and McClean had played upon his weakness not helped by his addiction to secret drinking and had used this to get his support for their schemes. In fact a year later Benjamin had to be dismissed being quite incapable of transacting business for the firm in a proper manner.

Apart from the many problems arising from this unfortunate affair, with hindsight it is obvious that the firm was unlikely to be able to fully recover from it but they were not to be deterred. That same year expenditure on special advertising was agreed. Some most attractive advertising products were issued from then onwards and there was a proliferation of fine things including calendars, diaries, atlases, dictionaries, and tins, many of these designed by artists of repute. There was even a small game, “Our Kings and Queens”, 38 cards produced in fine lithographic printing, a variation of “Happy Families”, the idea being to complete the “trick” of monarchs in each century. Many of these items are now much sought after by collectors.

[edit] Decline after 1918

Then came the 1914-1918 war and once again inevitably the tea tax was increased. John Lane Densham resigned the chairmanship in 1915 as his health had been failing for some time and he died whilst abroad on 13 February 1918. Unfortunately this meant that the firm had lost one of its main assets. Alexander Jackson took over as chairman and many amusing advertisements were still devised including the idea of using a small team of tame zebras to haul one of the Mazawattee vans around, notably in the Tunbridge Wells area. When he died in 1933 Joseph Alexander Densham (John Lane’s eldest son) became chairman but it was not long before the confectionery department had to be shut down (1936) to be followed shortly after by the start of the Second World War. This caused terrible destruction to the firm. Firstly in late 1940 the Tower Hill building was bombed and wrecked and if this was not bad enough, the New Cross factory was also heavily bombed and largely destroyed. In reality, this was the end of the business although it struggled on under Joseph Alexander Densham until he retired in 1953. The business was sold to a subsidiary of Burton, Son & Sanders Ltd, possibly as a tax-loss company. The Mazawattee name was used for a while although the tea was packed by one of the big tea firms but even that arrangement soon ended; the days of Mazawattee were over for good.

[edit] The Densham family and homes in Croydon and Purley.

When John Boon Densham (1814 - 1886) came to London from Plymouth, he may have lived in the Forest Hill area; in later years, perhaps when he had retired, he came to Croydon where his sons already lived. He lived at “Ferny Bank”, Hurst Road in the late 1870s and then moved to “Mannamead”, Birdhurst Road (now no.17) around 1881. He died there on 3 December 1886 aged 72 and was buried at St. John the Evangelist Church, Old Coulsdon on 8 December; his grave and that of his wife who had predeceased him (together with that of his eldest son Edward and his wife) is located to the west of the church.

Edward, who ran the firm for many years before the advent of the Mazawattee name, lived in Purley, initially at “Olden Lodge” in Olden Lane but in 1885 he bought a grand house, “Foxley”, at the Purley end of Higher Drive together with its large estate. In 1894 he was elected as an independent candidate to the new Croydon Rural District Council and was later Chairman for three years. He was also a J.P. being admired for his kindness. and was also a member and Chairman of the Board of Guardians. In this capacity he was present when the Duchess of Albany declared the Purley Fountain open (the Queen Victoria Memorial) on Saturday 11 June 1904. He became a man of some prominence locally and supported the introduction of electricity to the Croydon area. Many of his sons were educated at Whitgift School. “Foxley” had extensive grounds and woodland with pleasant views and around 1907 Edward set about using some of the land along the Higher Drive edge for new homes for his offspring: “Foxley Lodge” 2 Higher Drive (Arthur John); “Woodlands” no.31 (Edward D.); “Brackenhurst” no. 65 (Frank); “The Warren” no.67 (H.J. “Jack”); and “Tresco” no. 73 (George). Edward died on 30 April 1912 aged 69 and was buried in the same grave as his father and mother at Old Coulsdon. He left a gross estate of roughly £75,000 (£2,600,000 in current terms).

Alfred Densham lived at “Dingwall Cottage” Dingwall Road, Croydon but then moved slightly south to an area a number of the family soon settled in: St. Peter’s Road near St. Peter’s Church.. In the 1870s he was at 2 “Southbourne Villas”. Then in the 1890s he moved to “Hurstleigh” 23 South Park Hill Road and after his retirement in 1896 to “Foxley Lodge” 2 Higher Drive, Purley. Benjamin Densham, like Alfred, lived in the St. Peter’s Road area at “Britannia Villa”, (now no. 46), then moved to “Homeside” 26 Friends Road, “Sunnyside” 45 Birdhurst Rise, and in the 1890s to “Minard” 19 Chichester Road; but he was to outdo this when he moved to “Bramley Croft”, Haling Park Road, a splendid house. After the problems of 1907, he relocated to Hindhead and became a yachtsman.

John Lane Densham, who figured so much in the story of the Mazawattee firm, lived in Croydon, initially at “Springfield”, 7 Dingwall Road and then in the 1890s he joined the others in St. Peter’s Road, at “Deanfield” (no. 51). Around 1896 he moved to the magnificent “Waldronhyrst,” 24 The Waldrons; this fine house which was extended from time to time, needed a staff of some ten to run it including its own coachman. In 1904 he was elected to the Borough Council for three years and was also a governor of the Croydon General Hospital. John Lane must have led an adventurous life what with his prodigious efforts to build the firm up, no doubt not helped by his health which caused him to go abroad so much in days when travel was not as easy as it is today. His youngest son, Stephen Hugh, died in December 1917 at Étaples from wounds received near Arras and it was not long before John Lane died due to para-typhoid at the age of 64 whilst at the home of a married daughter in Boksburg, South Africa .

[edit] Remaining traces of the firm’s premises and homes

Remnants can still be found of this once famous firm. Admittedly, the imposing building at Tower Hill is no more due to bombing. The cellar areas remain in the form of “Tower Vaults”, a small largely underground shopping area with restaurants and small shops used mainly by visitors to the Tower of London; in the centre of the floor is a fine circular bronze plaque let into the floor commemorating the origin of the spot. The church of All Hallows by the Tower was also badly damaged in 1940 but was rebuilt later, so at last it has its original view to the east open to the light. At New Cross, the bombing was so bad that the factory was virtually wrecked yet some parts do remain. The words “Elizabeth Industrial Estate” can be spotted from trains on the way from New Cross Gate to London Bridge (just before the new Waste Transfer Station): these are on a smallish tower that was used as part of the factory boiler-system. The old Grand Surrey Canal is no more having been filled in and is now a straight stretch of road (the Surrey Canal Road). At the entrance to the Elizabeth Estate are a number of remnants of the old Mazawattee factory notably a corner building and part of the main entrance from the canal side.

Of John Boon Densham’s homes, only “Mannamead” remains as no. 17 Birdhurst Road, South Croydon. Edward’s fine “Foxley” (later “Foxley Hall”) became flats and then a hotel before being demolished around 1968 and replaced by flats and houses but still with a pleasant view to the east, and these retain the name “Foxley Hall”. His sons’ string of houses in Higher Drive largely remain in Higher Drive, well-maintained and fine properties. Only “Tresco” has gone - yet the name of the family has been thoughtfully given to the cul-de-sac, “Densham Drive” by the site. In the 1930s there was a proposal to develop the wooded area of the estate but there was a public outcry. This resulted in the purchase of 19 acres (7.688 hectares) by the by the Urban District Council in November 1937 and its registration under the Green Belt Scheme. Later Sherwood Oaks was added to this so that there is now one large area of public land.

Three of Alfred’s homes remain: 2 “Southbourne Villas” (now no. 15 St. Peter‘s Road), “Hurstleigh” 23 South Park Hill Road and “Foxley Lodge” 2 Higher Drive, only the Dingwall Road house having been demolished. Of Benjamin’s homes, two have gone (26 Friends Road and “Minard” 19 Chichester Road) but the others remain. “Bramley Croft” Haling Park Road is now “St. John’s Nursing Home”. The first of John Lane Densham’s houses in Dingwall Road has gone, but “Deanfield” is still there in St. Peter’s Road (no. 51); Waldronhyrst” became a residential hotel for many years. Known as The Waldronhyrst Hotel, it catered to visiting colonials from the sub continent until the late 1950's. It was then used partly as flats but was demolished in the 1970s to make way for a new estate of houses and flats, adjacent to “The Waldrons” conservation area.

Having covered the story of tea in Britain over some 350 years and how it affected the Densham family, a few well-chosen words by the poet William Cowper give a reminder of the perhaps more leisurely earlier days of tea drinking in the 18th century:

Now stir the fire, and close the shutters fast,
Let fall the curtains, wheel the sofa round,
And, while the bubbling and loud-hissing urn
Throws up a steamy column, and the cups
That cheer but not inebriate, wait on each,
So let us welcome peaceful ev’ning in.3

[edit] Trivia

In the 1940 film Gaslight a horse-drawn bus can be seen displaying a large advertisement for Mazawattee Tea. This occurs a few minutes into the film.
